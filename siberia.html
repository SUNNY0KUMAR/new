Daily Life in Exile

Daily Routine

  1. 4:00 AM - Wake-up call
  2. 4:30 AM - Roll call regardless of weather
  3. 5:00 AM - Breakfast (thin gruel or bread)
  4. 5:30 AM - March to work site, often kilometers away
  5. 6:00 AM - Begin labor shift
  6. 1:00 PM - Brief break for lunch (soup with occasional fish or vegetables)
  7. 7:00 PM - End work and return to camp
  8. 8:00 PM - Evening meal (bread ration based on work performance)
  9. 10:00 PM - Lights out

Survival Strategies

Those who survived the brutal conditions often relied on a combination of factors:

  • Strong social bonds with other prisoners, forming groups for mutual protection and resource sharing
  • Specialized skills that could earn placement in less physically demanding indoor work
  • Mental resilience and the ability to psychologically detach from immediate surroundings
  • Learning to navigate camp politics and developing relationships with those in positions of relative power
  • Adapting quickly to the unwritten rules and social hierarchies within the camp system

Survival was never guaranteed. Even the strongest and most resilient prisoners faced a daily battle against cold, hunger, exhaustion, and disease. Those who survived often carried physical and psychological scars for the remainder of their lives. Many who were eventually released found themselves unable to reintegrate into society, forever marked by their time in the "frozen wasteland" of Siberian exile.

The Frozen Wasteland

Siberia—a vast, unforgiving territory spanning over 13 million square kilometers—became the Soviet Union's most notorious prison without walls. This remote region, characterized by extreme cold, dense forests, and sparse population, was transformed into a vast network of labor camps where millions were exiled as enemies of the state.

The journey to Siberian exile was itself a form of torture. Prisoners were crammed into cattle trains with little food, water, or sanitation. These transports, known as "Stolypin cars," could take weeks to reach their remote destinations. Many prisoners died during transit from disease, exposure, or starvation before even reaching the camps.

What Awaits You?

  • Extreme Cold: Temperatures regularly drop below -50°C, with winter lasting up to 9 months. Frostbite and hypothermia were common causes of death and disability. Prisoners worked outdoors regardless of temperature, stopping only when it fell below -40°C.
  • Backbreaking Labor: Prisoners were forced to work 12-14 hour days in mining, logging, railroad construction, or industrial projects. Work quotas were deliberately set beyond human capacity, ensuring most prisoners failed to meet them and received reduced rations.
  • Starvation Rations: Food allocations were tied to work output. The standard daily ration included 300g of black bread, thin soup, and occasional fish or porridge—providing only 1,200-1,800 calories despite the extreme physical demands and cold.
  • Inadequate Shelter: Barracks were overcrowded, unheated, and infested with lice and bedbugs. In some camps, prisoners had to build their own housing after arrival, sleeping outdoors until construction was completed.
  • Disease and Minimal Medical Care: Tuberculosis, scurvy, pellagra, and dysentery were rampant. Medical facilities were primitive, with few medications and untrained personnel.

Major Siberian Camp Regions

Kolyma

Known as the "Land of White Death," Kolyma was the most dreaded destination. Gold mining operations here had the highest mortality rates. Located in the Far East, prisoners had to endure sea transport through the Arctic Ocean to reach it.

Norilsk

One of the northernmost cities in the world, built entirely by Gulag prisoners for nickel and copper mining. With nine months of winter and two months of polar night, conditions were extremely harsh.

Vorkuta

A coal mining center located north of the Arctic Circle. The area was so remote that escape was virtually impossible, surrounded by hundreds of miles of tundra with nowhere to hide.

Timeline of Siberian Exile

1930-1933

Mass Deportations

The first wave of mass deportations to Siberia targeted "kulaks" (wealthy peasants) who resisted collectivization. Entire families were exiled to special settlements.

1937-1938

Great Terror

During Stalin's purges, Siberian camps expanded rapidly. New industrial complexes were established in remote regions, built and operated entirely by prison labor.

1940-1946

Wartime Expansion

WWII led to further expansion of Siberian camps. Prisoners of war, ethnic minorities from border regions, and anyone suspected of collaboration were sent east.

1953-1956

Mass Releases

Following Stalin's death, millions were gradually released from Siberian exile, though many had nowhere to return to after years or decades away.
